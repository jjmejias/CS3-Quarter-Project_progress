What is 'Big Data'?

Big data primarily refers to data sets that are too large or complex to be dealt with by traditional data-processing application software. Data with many entries (rows) offer greater statistical power, while data with higher complexity (more attributes or columns) may lead to a higher false discover rate. Though used sometimes loosely partly because of a lack of formal definition, the interpretation that seems to best describe big data is the one associated with large body of information that we could not comprehend when used only in smaller amounts.

'Big Data Analytics'

Big data analytics is a form of advanced analytics, which involve complex applications with elements such as predictive models, statistical algorithms, and what-if analysis powered by analytics systems. Its process includes uncovering trends, patterns, and correlations against very large, diverse big data sets that include structured, semi-structured, and unstructured data, from different sources, and in different sizes from terabytes to zettabytes, to help make data-informed decisions.

History of 'Big Data'

Big Data originated with the rise of computing and relational databases in the 1970s. The advent of the internet and web in the 1990s led to an explosion of data. The development of Apache Hadoop in the 2000s enabled distributed storage and processing of large datasets. Big Data analytics emerged in the 2010s, leveraging advanced technologies like Apache Spark and NoSQL databases. The combination of Big Data and machine learning/AI has driven insights and automation. Today, Big Data is integral to industries, transforming decision-making and opening new possibilities for innovation and understanding.
